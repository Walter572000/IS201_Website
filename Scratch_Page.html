1. What Is a Key?

Think of a music key like a library.

  • In a library, books are sorted by genre—mystery, fantasy, history—so you know what kind of stories you'll find on each shelf.
  • If a song is in the key of C major, it's like saying “all these notes and chords belong in the C-major section of our library.”
  • Why this helps:
    • It gives us a clear “home shelf” (the I chord) and the list of other chords that live on the same shelf.
    • Just as you wouldn't expect a cookbook in the fantasy section, you wouldn't expect an F♯ chord in C major—it just wouldn't sound right!

2. What Is a Chord?

  • Imagine a choir of people. Each person sings a single note (a sound).
  • A chord is like a little choir: instead of one person, three (or more) sing together at the same time, making a richer sound.
  • If one person sings the note “C,” another sings “E,” and another “G,” together they form a C major chord—our “C-choir.”

3. What Is a Chord Progression?

  • Picture stepping stones across a stream. Each stone is a chord.
  • Walking in a pattern (stone 1 → stone 2 → stone 3 → stone 4) is like a chord progression.
  • Different patterns feel different—some feel happy, some feel sad, some feel exciting.

4. Meet I-V-vi-IV

  • I (one) is the “home” chord—where you start and feel at rest.
  • V (five) wants to move back to home—it's a little push.
  • vi (six minor) feels a bit sad or gentle.
  • IV (four) feels like a friendly detour before heading home.

Put them together: I → V → vi → IV

In the key of C major:

  1. I = C Major (people singing C-E-G)
  2. V = G Major (G-B-D)
  3. vi = A Minor (A-C-E)
  4. IV = F Major (F-A-C)

6. Practice Exercise

Time to get your fingers moving!

  1. Strumming Pattern
    • Try this simple, pop-friendly beat: Down → Down-Up → Up-Down-Up
    • Count it as “1 (down), and-a (down-up), 2-and-a (up-down-up).”
    • Keep your wrist loose and play slowly at first—one chord per count.
  2. Loop the Progression
    • Play C → G → Am → F four times in a row.
    • Start at 60~BPM on a metronome or drum track, then speed up as you get comfortable.
